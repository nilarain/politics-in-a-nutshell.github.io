EU

  • What is it? European countries that participate in the world economy under one currency, the Euro.
  • History: The EU began as European Coal and Steel Community (ECSC) and the European Economic Community (EEC), formed by Northern European countries to make trade more efficient. The EEC was known as the Common Market in English speaking countries. Following the “Fight on Terror” in 2001 many countries within the EU began combating terror attacks. Later, the economic downfall of 2008 put strains in an already struggling EU.
  • Accomplishments: Their accomplishments include providing safe and cheap transport throughout Europe, standardizing the Euro and making a health insurance card usable in any EU territory
  • Recent Events: The EU provides ability to move through borders freely, and with the growing numbers of refugees pouring into Europe, Britain became scared that a refugee could possibly be a future terrorist. The country voted and it resulted in Great Britain abandoning the European Union (knows as the “Brexit” in 2016).

NAFTA

  • What is it? The purpose of NAFTA was to create thousands of jobs for Americans. However, nearly 700,000 jobs have been displaced due to the American-Mexican trade deficit of $60 billion, which is also the main reason for unemployment. Before NAFTA took place, the US had a $1.6 billion dollar surplus with Mexico. All 50 states, the District of Columbia and Puerto Rico have had jobs displaced to Mexico.
  • Recent Events: NAFTA has recently (2011) included Colombia, South Korea, and Panama into the trade agreement. Multiple protests arose from the instability of jobs that would come. Many labor unions have filed complaints on labor practices directed at multiple countries; such as Mexico, Honduras, and Guatemala. Also, a huge reason for Mexican immigrants entering the US is the poor working conditions set by NAFTA.

TPP

  • What is it? The Trans-Pacific Partnership is a pact between 12 countries: United States, Canada, Mexico, Peru, Chile, Japan, Vietnam, Malaysia, Singapore, Australia, Vietnam and New Zealand. The TPP aims to deepen economic ties between these nations, slashing tariffs and fostering trade to boost growth. These countries are hoping the partnership will create a closer bond on economic policies and regulation.
  • History: TPP started with the P4 trade agreement between four nations, (Brunei, Chile, New Zealand and Singapore), that came into effect in 2006. The original deal removed tariffs on most goods traded between the countries, promising to co-operate on wider issues such as employment practices, intellectual property and competition policies.
    • Pros
    Cons
    Seeks to govern exchange of not only traditional goods and services, but also intellectual property and foreign investment; would promote trade in knowledge-intensive services in which companies exert a strong comparative advantage. Certain proposed patent and copyright rules in the deal could keep the cost of medicine around the world higher than it should be.
    It’s argued that killing the TPP would do little to bring factory work back to America or other countries involved and the potential gains of the TPP for the partners are significant. According to leaked drafts, the proposed pact would ban state and local governments from extending preferences to vendors of locally-produced goods and services. "Buy American" programs would be forbidden. Local employers could not be supported. Local farmers could not be favored.
    Some have argued that although China is not part of the TPP, enacting the agreement would raise regulatory rules and standards for several of China’s key trading partners. That would pressure China to meet some of those standards and cease its attempts to game global trade to impede foreign multinational companies. All TPP negotiations to date have been conducted behind closed doors. No information has been released to the press and the public; no public participation or comment is invited or allowed. Even U.S. Senators have been barred from seeing negotiation points or drafts.
